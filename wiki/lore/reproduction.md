## Overview

One of the most common questions asked of the new world is a practical one: where do all the [toys](toys) come from? The short answer is that the toy population is not left to chance — it is **manufactured and managed**, and the institution behind it is [SinCorp](sincorp). The longer answer is more reassuring than the question usually expects: after a brutal early collapse, the toy population **stabilized**, and toys today are not a dwindling rarity but a plentiful, renewable part of the world.

The [giantesses](the-transformed) barely factor into the question at all. The [serum](apotheosis-serum) nearly halted their ageing, so the ruling class scarcely needs replacing; the women who came through [TDay](tday) are, for practical purposes, still the women who hold the world. Population, as a thing that has to be actively maintained, is a *toy* matter — and it is a solved one.

## From collapse to stability

It was not always solved. In the immediate [Post-Apotheosis](era-post-apotheosis) chaos and on into the early [Reconstruction](era-the-reconstruction), the toy population cratered — enormous numbers lost in the disorder of a world remaking itself, faster than anything could replace them. For a time it genuinely looked as though toys might become scarce.

What turned it around was industry. Once SinCorp perfected the means to **grow toys in its vats** and to have **male toys themselves carry and bear** new stock, replacement finally outpaced loss. The freefall levelled off, the numbers steadied, and the crisis passed into history. The world that exists now is the world on the other side of that recovery: one where the supply of toys is dependable, controlled, and — by any measure that matters — abundant.

## A manufactured, stable supply

Toy production is, in the end, an **industry**, and SinCorp owns it end to end. Two threads carry it:

The first is **vat-grown stock** — toys produced directly in SinCorp facilities and matured to adulthood outside any natural birth. This is the backbone: a supply SinCorp can scale to demand, wholly independent of who is with whom out in the cities.

The second runs through the [toy modifications](toy-modifications) catalogue, which includes the means to make toys themselves **carry and bear** SinCorp's stock — folding the toy population into the very machinery that renews it.

Between the vats and the carriers, SinCorp holds every lever of how many toys exist, of what quality, and where — and it keeps those numbers comfortably ahead of what the world spends.

## Abundance

The clearest proof of how thoroughly the shortage ended is how *casually* toys are now treated as a resource. They are common enough to be more than servants and companions: shrunk down, a toy is a commodity like any other, and toys are plentiful enough that even being **used as food** — a shrunken delicacy — is an ordinary, unremarkable part of the world's economy rather than a scandalous waste. A civilisation only spends a population that way when it is no longer afraid of losing it. Yet plenty is not the same as endless: demand for toys is effectively bottomless, and a supply that never runs dry still can never quite keep pace with an appetite that never eases. The toy world is not a dying one — but it is one in which toys remain, always, a resource worth managing.

## Wild-caught: the luxury exception

Against all that manufactured abundance, one kind of toy stands apart as genuinely rare: the **wild-caught**. Toys taken from the [Wilds](the-wilds) by the [Huntresses](the-paws) — survivors, self-made, shaped by a life no vat can reproduce — command a steep premium precisely because everything else is so plentiful. A farmed toy is the everyday stock of the world; a wild-caught toy is a luxury, a prize, a mark of the owner as much as the owned. It is the one corner of the toy trade where rarity, not volume, sets the terms.

## The life of a toy is not one thing

It is worth saying plainly, because the world's harsher edges get the most attention: a toy's fate is not a single grim story. Toys are spent carelessly in some places, yes — the world can afford to, and the [Wilds](the-wilds) and [R-Labs](r-labs) are real. But abundance cuts both ways. A resource that is plentiful is also *ordinary*, woven into daily life, and an enormous number of toys live full, long, and even comfortable lives: kept by owners who value them, settled into households, prized, indulged, and in no hurry to meet any dramatic end. The new world is cruel by design, but it is not uniformly or inescapably fatal to be a toy in it — there is as much room in the setting for a toy's whole life story as for a short one.

## A supply, and a leash

Owning the means of toy production hands SinCorp a power no single [Matriarch](matriarchs) can match. Every [mega-city](mega-cities) runs on toys, and every mega-city, in the end, gets its toys — stock, replacements, the mods that keep them useful — from SinCorp. A Matriarch rules her city absolutely, yet she does not manufacture her own population, and that one dependency threads SinCorp's influence into every city in the world. The toy supply is not only a demographic fact; it is one of the quiet leashes by which a decentralised world stays tied to SinCorp.

## The Resistance

With the population long since stable, the [Resistance](the-resistance) is not, and has never really been, about preventing toys from dying out. Its cause is simpler and more human: toys are people, and property, and the Resistance moves particular ones out of the cities toward [Bastion](bastion) because those particular ones deserve a life of their own choosing. It does not aim to shut down the vats or change how the supply is made. It aims to change what happens to the individuals caught inside it — which, with the numbers long since holding steady, is a fight about freedom rather than survival.

## Did You Know?

- The toy population **crashed** in the Post-Apotheosis chaos and early Reconstruction — but **stabilized** once SinCorp perfected vat-growing and toy-carried gestation. The decline is history, not the present.
- Toys today are **abundant**, not scarce — plentiful enough that shrunken toys are an ordinary commodity, used even as a delicacy.
- **Wild-caught** toys are the luxury exception: rare and prized precisely because farmed toys are so common.
- The [giantesses](the-transformed) barely reproduce at all — the serum nearly halted their ageing — so maintaining the population is almost entirely a *toy* matter, and a solved one.
- Because every city depends on SinCorp for its toys, toy production is one of the main ways a decentralised world stays tethered to SinCorp's power.
- A toy's life is **not one fixed story** — the world can be harsh, but abundance also means many toys live long, settled, even cherished lives.
