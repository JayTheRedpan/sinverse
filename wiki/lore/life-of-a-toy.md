## Overview

This page concerns the daily reality of [toys](toys) living within the [mega-cities](mega-cities) — the majority of the toy population, and the ones whose lives are most fully shaped by the new world order. For what it means to live as a toy *outside* the cities, see the [Wild Toys](wild-toys); for what a toy *is*, see [Toys](toys).

There is no single life of a city toy, because a toy is not a citizen with a defined place but a possession whose entire existence depends on who owns him and what that owner wants. The same world that is, from above, a kind of dark utopia — a society where the dominant gender's every appetite is provided for and the natural order has at last been set right — is, from below, the narrowed life of a thing that may be cherished, worked, displayed, studied, or discarded entirely at another's pleasure. Both readings are true at once, and the gap between them is the whole of a toy's situation. The giantesses do not, for the most part, experience their world as cruel. They experience it as *correct*. That conviction is what makes it what it is.

A toy's life in the cities tends to fall into one of several broad roles, ranging from the cherished to the consumed to the merely installed. A single toy may pass through more than one of them over time, and the line between the best of them and the worst can be a single bad day.

## Companions

At the most fortunate end are the kept toys — the companions, pets, and favourites of individual giantesses who want a particular toy and mean to keep him.

To a giantess, a beloved companion toy is one of the genuine pleasures of the new world: something small and precious to dote on, dress up, indulge, and adore, all the more satisfying for how completely it depends on her. The affection can be entirely real. So can the care — a prized companion is fed well, housed comfortably, protected, often modified by SinCorp to survive her safely, and treated, in the giantess's own mind, far better than a toy has any right to expect. From above, this is kindness, even love.

From below, it is love with no floor under it. A companion's good life exists entirely at his owner's pleasure and lasts exactly as long as her interest does; the same hand that dotes can discard, and the affection that keeps him comfortable is the affection of someone who owns a pet, not someone who recognises a peer. The kindest version of a toy's life is still a life in which he has no say, no recourse, and no guarantee of tomorrow. It is, by a wide margin, the best a city toy can hope for — and that is itself the bleakest thing about it.

## Servants

Far more common is the working toy: the domestic servant kept to cook, clean, fetch, carry, and maintain a household or a business. Toys are abundant labour in a world that no longer values their lives, and a great many spend their days in service — running homes, staffing the establishments of the cities, doing the small-scale work that giantess hands are too large to bother with.

To the giantesses, this is simply the sensible use of an available resource; toys exist, they are good for tasks, and putting them to those tasks is no more remarkable than any other household arrangement. A servant toy with a reasonable owner may have something approaching stability — a routine, a function, a reason to be kept around that does not depend purely on a giantess's mood.

For the toy, that function is also the whole of his security. His value is his usefulness, and usefulness is conditional: the servant who stays useful stays, and the one who breaks, ages, slows, or displeases is replaced without ceremony from a population the cities still treat as renewable. Service is the broad, grey middle of a city toy's possible lives — neither the indulgence of the companion nor the horror of the worst tiers, but a quiet, permanent precariousness, a life held by the thread of remaining worth the trouble.

## City Service

Distinct from the household servant is the toy in service to the *city itself*. Mega-cities run on enormous, unglamorous labour — maintaining infrastructure, working the systems SinCorp threads through everything, keeping the vast machinery of a giantess-scale metropolis functioning at the small scale where giantess hands cannot reach. Much of that work is done by organised crews of toys: pooled, housed together, and assigned to the tasks that keep the lights on, the water moving, and the city standing.

These crews are overseen — and in the cities' culture, a crew of working toys is understood to belong, for all practical purposes, to whoever oversees it. Traditionally the overseer is a giantess; increasingly, in the more modern cities, the role falls to a [Sylph](project-sylph), the lieutenant class engineered precisely for this kind of middle authority. The overseer directs the work, answers for the crew's output, and enjoys a perk the arrangement openly includes: the toys under her command are hers to use as she sees fit. Authority over a labour crew is, by unspoken custom, authority over the bodies in it.

To the city, this is simply efficient administration — a resource organised and put to productive use, with a clear line of responsibility. To the toy, city service is a life of labour with no household's relative shelter around it: he is one of many, valued in aggregate rather than individually, kept by the city's need for hands rather than any owner's particular regard, and subject at all hours to an overseer who holds both his workload and his body at her discretion. It is steady in a way the worst tiers are not, and impersonal in a way that offers a different kind of coldness — to be necessary, but never to anyone in particular.

## Objects

If the city-service toy *maintains* the city, the object toy *becomes* it. This is the most complete literalization of the word *toy* the new world produces: males repurposed not as labour or entertainment but as fixtures — bound, fitted, or installed into the fabric of a building or a public space to serve a function, the way one would mount a fitting or place a piece of furniture.

The forms it takes are macabre by the standards of any older world and unremarkable by the standards of this one: a toy set into a wall or a post to be used in passing; one fixed in place as a fitting, a holder, a handle, a seat; one made a permanent feature of a parlour, an establishment, or a private residence, present and aware but reduced to the role of a thing in a room. SinCorp's [modifications](toy-modifications) are what make it survivable — the durability, the endurance, the regenerative and oxygen-sustaining enhancements that let a body persist in a role no unaltered person could outlast. A toy can be kept as an object for a very long time.

To the giantesses, this is decor and convenience with a frisson the old world's furniture never had — an appointment that knows it is an appointment, a fixture that can be made to respond. To install a toy as part of one's home or business is a statement of the order itself: proof that a male's highest possible use is to be made literally useful, and that the line between a person and a possession was never real to begin with. To the toy, it is the quietest and most total erasure on the entire spectrum — not killed, not even quite used in the active sense, but simply *placed*, his existence narrowed to a function and a location, aware the whole time of exactly what he has been made into. It sits among the bleakest of the castes precisely because it does not end him. It keeps him, and keeps him knowing.

## Entertainment

A great many city toys exist for use and spectacle — the toys of the establishments, the pleasure-houses, the markets, and the private appetites the new world is built to satisfy. This is the role the word *toy* most directly names, and it is where the dark-utopia framing is at its most unembarrassed.

For the giantesses, the open, casual, abundant availability of toys for use is one of the defining luxuries of the new era — appetites the serum left burning hot, met without shame or scarcity of conscience, in a society that regards doing so as natural and good. The [empathy drop](the-transformed) leaves a giantess with no inner objection to anything she might want from a toy, and rewards the exercise of dominion in whatever form she takes it; the entertainment tier is simply where that is serviced most openly, and where the giantesses who take it in its cruellest forms are most freely indulged.

For the toy, it is the tier where his personhood is least acknowledged and his survival least assured. Used toys are spent, sometimes carefully and sometimes not at all, and in the harsher cities and venues a toy in this role lives close to the edge that the early post-[TDay](tday) years made infamous — the grab-use-discard cycle that the toys' own value has only partly reined in. That the cities now have economic reason to *not* burn through their toys quite so fast is, for a toy in the entertainment tier, the thinnest imaginable comfort.

## Research Subjects

Some toys disappear into SinCorp. Every mega-city's [SinCorp](sincorp) branch runs its own research, much of it undisclosed, and toys are its raw material — the population on which [modifications](toy-modifications) are developed, refined, and tested before they reach the market, and on which the company's stranger and darker experiments are conducted.

To SinCorp, this is progress: the work that produces the very modifications keeping companion toys alive, the [reproduction](reproduction) technology sustaining the population, and the advances the new world runs on. It is framed, where it is framed at all, as toys serving a purpose larger than themselves.

To the toy, being taken for research is among the worst fates the cities hold, precisely because it is the least visible and the least bounded. A research toy's life is defined by whatever is being done to him, by people who record him under a clinical designation rather than a name, behind doors that — in the central complex at [Angora](angora) — are not filed with any city authority at all. Toys taken deep into SinCorp's work are, as a rule, not seen again.

## Food

The bleakest of the tiers is the one in which a toy is not used but *consumed*. The new world is full of predators — the [macro](macro) [Transformed](the-transformed), the [Overgrown](the-overgrown) of the Wilds, and giantesses whose appetites simply run that way — and a toy small enough in scale, whether naturally diminutive or deliberately shrunk down with SinCorp's [shrinking technology](toy-modifications), can become food in the most literal sense. Some toys are reduced specifically for it; some are simply small enough already.

To the giantesses who indulge it, this is one more appetite the new order is free to satisfy without shame — a thing the old world would have called monstrous and the new one regards as merely a taste, no more remarkable than any other use a toy might be put to. It is not universal, and many cities and individuals consider it wasteful given how prized toys have become, but it is openly part of the spectrum of what a toy may be *for*.

For the toy, it is the end past which there is no worse tier. There is no security in this caste, no usefulness to preserve, no owner whose regard for her property offers a thread of protection — only the role of a thing that exists to be eaten. It is the absolute floor of a toy's possible existence, and its presence in the order is part of what gives every tier above it its edge: a toy who slips down the ladder of usefulness or favour always knows what waits at the bottom of it.

## Strays

At the bottom are the toys who belong to no one: the strays, the escaped, the abandoned, the black-market merchandise of the lower districts. An unowned toy in a city occupies a strange and dangerous limbo — free of any single owner, and therefore free of the one thing that offers a city toy any protection at all.

A toy's only real shield in the cities is *ownership*: a collar marks him as someone's property, and to damage another giantess's property carries consequences a giantess will think about. The stray has no such mark. He belongs to no one, which means anyone may do as she likes with him and answer to no one for it — and it also means he is fair game to be claimed, sold, or swept up into any of the tiers above. Strays survive in the cracks of the cities, in the underdistricts and black markets, always one encounter from being owned, used, or worse. The collar that the owned toy wears as the symbol of his subjugation is, to the stray, the very thing he lacks — and the bitter irony at the heart of a toy's life in the cities is that protection and ownership are the same thing.

Which raises the obvious question: why does a stray stay? An unowned toy is not chained to the city, and the [Wilds](the-wilds) lie open beyond its walls. The answer, for most, is fear. The Wilds are a place of the [Overgrown](the-overgrown) — building-sized predators, predatory flora, and loosed experiments — and the professional hunters of the [Paws](the-paws) — a country that kills constantly and indifferently, where the stories that reach the cities are uniformly horrifying. To a toy who has only ever known the cities, the devil he knows is the one that, at least, runs on rules he understands: stay small, stay unseen, stay in the cracks, and the city's dangers are survivable in a way the open Wilds seem not to be. Most strays calculate, rightly or wrongly, that a hunted life in the cracks of a city is better odds than the green hell outside — and so they stay, enduring the limbo rather than risking the crossing. It is the same fear that keeps the cities' grip on their toys firm without needing walls to hold them: for most, there is simply nowhere safer to run.

## The Collar and the Order

Running through every tier is the simple machinery of ownership. A toy's place is defined not by law — toys have no standing in any city's law — but by whose he is. The collar, the tag, the mark of an owner: these are at once the instruments of his subjugation and the only protection he will ever have, because the new world extends its single mercy, such as it is, only to property that belongs to someone. An owned toy is shielded by his owner's regard for her own possessions. A toy who belongs to no one is shielded by nothing.

This is the order the giantesses built and believe in: not, to their minds, a cruelty imposed on a people, but the natural arrangement of a world finally set right, in which the lesser sex occupies its proper place and the dominant one is at last free to live as it pleases. That the lesser sex experiences this as the narrowing of every life to the dimensions of someone else's want is not, for most giantesses, a thing that registers — and where it does register, the serum has seen to it that it mostly does not weigh. The exceptions are the [Tempered](the-resistance), who feel the gap between the two readings and cannot stop feeling it, and who make up much of the [Resistance](the-resistance) as a result.

## Did You Know

- A toy's single greatest protection in the cities is being owned. A collar marks him as property, and damaging another giantess's property carries consequences — which is exactly why an unowned stray is in many ways more exposed than an owned toy.
- The same arrangement is experienced as a dark utopia from above and a bleak reality from below. To most giantesses the new order is not cruelty but correctness — the world set right — and that sincere conviction is much of what sustains it.
- The kindest life a city toy can have, as a cherished companion, still rests entirely on his owner's continued interest. The best case and the worst case are separated by a change of mind.
- No two cities treat their toys alike. The same act that is unremarkable in one mega-city may cost a toy his life, or cost a giantess her standing, in another.
- Toys taken into SinCorp's research are recorded under clinical designations rather than names, and those taken deepest into the work are generally not seen again.
- The toys' worth, not mercy, is what has reined in the disposable excess of the early years. The cities conserve their toys for the same reason they once squandered them: it is what serves the giantesses best.
- Crews of toys keep the cities physically running, and a crew is understood to belong to whoever oversees it — a giantess, or increasingly a Sylph, who may use the toys under her command as freely as she directs their work.
- The most literal use of the word *toy* is the object caste: males installed as fixtures and furnishings, kept alive in the role for years by SinCorp modifications. It is among the bleakest fates precisely because it does not end the toy — it keeps him, aware, as a thing.
- At the very bottom of the order is food: toys small enough, or shrunk down far enough, to be eaten. Its place on the spectrum of what a toy may be *for* is part of what gives every tier above it its edge.
- Most strays could, in theory, flee to the Wilds — and most never do. The terror of what lives out there keeps the cities' grip on their toys firm without any need for walls.
